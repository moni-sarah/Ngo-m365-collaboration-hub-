01

Client context

The client is a Geneva-based international NGO operating across multiple regions with a headquarters team of 40–80 staff and field offices in five countries. The organisation qualifies for and holds a Microsoft 365 Nonprofit Business Premium subscription, providing access to the full M365 suite at steeply discounted rates — a common profile across the Geneva international organisation ecosystem.

Like the majority of NGOs at this scale, the organisation had active Microsoft 365 licensing but negligible technical capacity to configure it. SharePoint existed but was unused. Teams had been adopted informally, producing 30+ ad-hoc workspaces with no governance. Staff communicated across personal email, WhatsApp groups, and shared drives, with no single source of truth for policies, projects, or institutional knowledge.

Sector context
Geneva hosts over 750 international organisations and NGOs. Almost all qualify for Microsoft Nonprofit licensing. The gap between licensing entitlement and actual platform utilisation is consistently large — creating a well-defined consulting opportunity for M365 specialists with NGO sector knowledge.

02

Problem statement

The organisation faced three compounding operational problems, each solvable with existing M365 tooling — none of which had been configured.

1

Fragmented communication, no institutional memory

Staff communicated across WhatsApp groups, personal email, and ad-hoc Teams. No central location for announcements, project status, or policy documents. New staff onboarding required weeks of individual briefings. Critical knowledge existed only in people's heads or personal inboxes.

2

Teams sprawl with no governance

Over 30 Teams workspaces had been created ad-hoc by staff over 18 months. No naming convention, no ownership policy, no archiving process. Duplicate workspaces for the same project existed simultaneously. Staff did not know which workspace was current.

3

Unstructured SharePoint with no findable content

SharePoint had been provisioned but never structured. Documents were uploaded with no metadata, no library taxonomy, and no content ownership. The search function returned unreliable results. Staff continued using personal OneDrive or emailing attachments instead.

Key diagnostic finding
None of these problems required new software. Every solution was available in the existing M365 nonprofit licence. The gap was configuration, governance design, and organisational change management — not technology acquisition.
03

Consulting approach

The engagement was structured as a fixed-scope, fixed-timeline delivery. Before any build work began, governance decisions were forced to a resolution — a discipline most internal IT projects skip, and the primary reason NGO intranet projects fail within 18 months of launch.

Three principles governed every technical decision made during the engagement:

🔒

Native tooling first

Zero custom code for MVP. Every requirement was satisfied with native M365 web parts and connectors. This ensures the client can maintain the solution independently after handover.

📋

Governance before build

No site was provisioned without a named content owner. No library was built without a defined metadata taxonomy. Governance decisions were client-signed before each phase gate.

🎯

Zero additional cost

All solution components were verified against the client's existing M365 nonprofit licence tier before scoping. No new subscriptions were introduced at any stage of the engagement.

04

Solution architecture

The solution is built on a hub-and-spoke SharePoint model integrated with a governed Teams workspace structure. One global hub site owns navigation, branding, and search scope. Four associated sites carry department-level content. Staff see a unified intranet but each team controls their own pages independently.

🌐 Global hub site — intranet.organisation.sharepoint.com
├── Homepage (4-zone launchpad + news feed)
├── Tools & apps Native web parts
└── Quick links: Teams, OneDrive, HR system, finance portal, helpdesk, grants portal
├── Knowledge hub Owned: IT/Ops lead
├── HR policies & procedures (EN + FR)
├── IT guides (MFA, VPN, M365 setup)
└── Onboarding portal — day-1 guide, org chart, glossary
├── Org news & community Owned: Comms lead
├── Announcements (SharePoint News web part)
├── Field stories — success stories from programme staff
└── Events calendar — org-wide, GMT+1
└── Programme library Associated site
├── Programme A / B / C individual pages
├── Cross-cutting: M&E templates, comms assets, finance forms
└── Archive — closed programmes, searchable only, not in nav

🏢 Associated department sites
├── Finance & grants Restricted library
└── Budget trackers, grant register, compliance docs, sensitive file library
├── Programmes & impact Per-programme sections
└── Project pages, reporting templates, field updates per programme team
├── HR & operations Restricted employment records
└── Staff directory, leave forms, travel policy, benefits, employment records (restricted)
└── IT & systems IT consultant owned
└── Helpdesk portal, system status, M365 guides, IT asset register
Hub site association — why it matters
Designating the intranet homepage as a SharePoint hub site enables unified navigation across all associated sites, cross-site search, and consistent branding — without requiring all content to live on one site. This is the architectural decision that makes the intranet scalable and maintainable by non-technical staff.
05

Teams governance

Teams sprawl is the single most common failure mode in NGO M365 deployments. The first intervention was to restrict Teams creation to IT admin only — enforced via Azure AD policy — and to define a canonical workspace register before provisioning a single new Team.

All 30+ existing ad-hoc Teams were audited: active Teams were migrated to the standard template, inactive Teams (no activity in 90 days) were archived.

Governance controls configured in Teams admin center:

Team creation policy

Restricted to IT admin only. Staff submit workspace requests via a Power Apps form. Prevents sprawl from day one.

Naming convention

Format: [DEPT]-[PURPOSE]
e.g. FIN-GrantPipeline, PROG-MalariaProject. Enforced via Azure AD naming policy.

Expiry policy

90-day inactivity triggers a renewal notification to the workspace owner. Unrenewed workspaces are archived, not deleted.

Guest access

Off at tenant level. Enabled per-Team via IT request only. Guests access assigned Teams only — no SharePoint intranet access unless explicitly granted.

Standard programme channel template
Every programme Team was provisioned with four standard channels: General (pinned: SharePoint programme page + Planner board), Field Updates, Reporting, and Resources (pinned SharePoint document library tab). Staff cannot add channels without IT approval — this prevents the fragmentation that destroyed the previous workspace structure.

Multilingual configuration (EN/FR)
SharePoint's native multilingual feature was enabled during initial build — not retrofitted. English set as default. French translation pages created for: Homepage, Knowledge Hub, Onboarding, and all HR policy pages. A bilingual staff member was designated as translation approver. Programme pages remain English-only unless a French-speaking programme team requests translation.

Critical governance decision — external access
Member organisations, partner consultants, and affiliate contacts were explicitly excluded from the main intranet. External contacts were routed to a separate SharePoint extranet or Teams guest access. Mixing internal staff and external contacts on the same SharePoint tenant is the most common governance error in NGO intranet projects — and the most consequential for data protection.

Do / don't rules for NGO M365 projects

These rules are distilled from the engagement and from patterns observed across multiple Geneva NGO M365 deployments. They are the difference between a solution that outlasts the consultant and one that is abandoned within 18 months.

Do
Define a named content owner per section before building. An unowned section is unmaintained.
Don't
Give member organisations or partners access to the main intranet. External contacts belong in a separate extranet.
Do
Use hub site association for all department sites. This enables unified search, consistent navigation, and shared branding.
Don't
Build the intranet as a single flat SharePoint site. It becomes unmanageable at scale and breaks search scope.
Do
Restrict Teams creation to IT admin on day one. Retroactive cleanup of sprawl costs more than upfront governance.
Don't
Migrate documents without reviewing them first. Migrating the old mess into a new location reproduces the problem.
Do
Mirror Azure AD groups in SharePoint permissions. Every joiners/leavers event then cascades automatically.
Don't
Build custom SPFx code for MVP. Native web parts deliver all requirements and ensure post-handover independence.
Do
Enable multilingual during build, not after. Retrofitting translation to an existing site is significantly more work.
Don't
Launch without the site ownership register signed. No named owner means no maintained content. The site stales immediately.
Do
Pilot with 4–5 staff before all-staff launch. Small-pilot feedback is categorically different from post-launch feedback.
Don't
Use internal acronyms or department codes in navigation labels. Labels must be plain language for a new joiner on their first day.
Replication note
This architecture is replicable across any Geneva-based NGO or international organisation holding a Microsoft 365 Nonprofit licence at Business Basic tier or above. The pattern scales from 20-person associations to 500-person secretariats with structural adjustments to the hub-spoke depth only.
